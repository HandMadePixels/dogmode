Canine Influenza

October 2009
DogMode Team Leaders, along with a Salt Lake Vet Services Technician, attended a seminar on the Canine Influenza on October 15, 2009. This seminar was hosted by Kimberly Wilmert with Intervet/Schering-Plough Animal Health and Central Valley Veterinary Clinic.

The topic of the seminar was the origin and spread of the disease among our canine friends, diagnosing and treating, and the H3N8 Flu Vaccine. The attendees will be providing the updated information to the entire DogMode Team over the next week. As of October 16, 2009, Clients can request educational material on this topic at the DogMode front counter.

After receiving this information, DogMode simply recommends that you take the time to discuss Canine Influenza with your Vet and educate yourself on the illness and vaccine. We will continue to acquire information from a handful of Vets in our community to determine whether or not we should require this vaccine as a business.

Please note that the Canine Flu can not be contracted from canines to humans. As always, thank you for being caring and responsible dog guardians.

Bark on!




November 2006
Everyone should be aware of this relatively new virus.

This virus started in the Greyhound Race tracks and has gradually been moving all around the country. It has been confirmed that this virus is now in Utah as well as many other states.

Dogs do not have natural immunity for it and there is no vaccine available at this time.

The symptoms are very similar to those of Kennel Cough, which causes the frequent misdiagnosis of Canine Influenza. Dog owners should watch their dogs closely for signs such as: coughing, increased respiration, gagging and high temperature.

Fortunately, if treatment is started as soon as symptoms are noticed, most dogs recover with no problems. As with any kind of virus, dogs who are diabetic, very old, very young, or undergoing chemotherapy are more at risk, as are dogs with other diseases such as Addison’s disease. Treatment includes prescribing antibiotics to prevent any secondary bacterial infections that can be dangerous.

In addition to DogMode’s standard cleaning and disinfecting procedure which is performed TWICE daily with an automated floor scrubber that applies 79 pounds of scrubbing pressure while applying clean disinfectant the entire time - plus the effective scrubbing and hosing of disinfectant in all of DogMode’s drain-system kennels daily; some systems DogMode has in place to further eliminate / contain the spread of illness include the creation of 3 separate zones for dogs.




An example of how DogMode can utilize the different zones is as follows:

Zone 1: A fully contained daycare area with solid walls and separate ventilation system for dogs that we are 90% sure are healthy. The dogs are not showing signs or symptoms and have not been showing signs or symptoms for at least 5 days.

Zone 2: A fully contained daycare area with solid walls and separate ventilation system for dogs that are not showing symptoms but due to the 1 - 5 day incubation period, we are not positive they have not been infected somewhere, Dog Park, Vet Office, anywhere.

Zone 3: A fully contained isolation area with solid walls and separate ventilation system for dogs that are showing signs and symptoms.

DogMode has also set up a decontamination process for employees when leaving the Zone where the dogs showing signs and symptoms are located. They have clothing they wear over their standard clothing which they remove in the decontamination zone where they disinfect shoes, hands, face, etc before entering the clean or safe zone.

The Department of Agriculture and Food agrees that the systems DogMode has in place are the correct way to be handling this kind of situation. This kind of system to contain and reduce as much transmission from dog to dog as possible is paramount in getting and keeping this under control. All Boarding Facilities, Daycare Facilities, Grooming Facilities, and Vet offices should be taking steps like this.
